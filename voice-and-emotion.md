# THE VITTORI LETTER — THE RAW VERSION
## What the newsletter actually needs to feel like — with examples you can taste.

---

## Forget strategy for a second. What does this newsletter need to DO to a person?

It needs to make them feel something they didn't expect to feel about a car they can't afford. It needs to make them text a friend. It needs to haunt them a little.

The strategy doc was right about the frameworks. But frameworks don't make people forward emails at 2am. **Stories do. Tension does. Beauty does. Uncomfortable truths do.**

So here's the raw version. Not what the newsletter should be *about*. What it should *feel like*.

---

## The goal isn't "our readers are informed."
## The goal is: **"I can't stop thinking about the old man with the crankshaft."**

---

## The 6 Emotions Every Edition Should Hit At Least One Of

Not "intellectual stimulation." That's a boardroom word for something much more primal:

| Emotion | What It Feels Like |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Holy Shit** | "I had no idea this existed / was happening / was possible" |
| **Beautiful Sadness** | "Something irreplaceable is disappearing and most people don't know" |
| **Urgency** | "There's a window closing and the people who see it early will be different" |
| **Reframe** | "I thought I understood this thing but I was looking at it completely wrong" |
| **Righteous Anger** | "Something important is being done badly by people who should know better" |
| **Quiet Reverence** | "There are people in this world who have devoted their entire lives to something most of us have never thought about" |

Edition 03 hit **Beautiful Sadness + Quiet Reverence + Urgency** simultaneously. That's why it worked. Enzo with the crankshaft isn't "content." It's a story that makes you feel something true about being human in a world that's automating everything.

Edition 02 hit none of them. It was interesting. Interesting doesn't get forwarded.

## What Dead Content Sounds Like vs. What Alive Content Sounds Like

### DEAD (Informative but forgettable)
> "Divergent Technologies has open-sourced the topology parameters from the 21C chassis. This represents a significant advancement in additive manufacturing data availability."

*Reader action: skim, close, forget.*

### ALIVE (Edition 03 already does this)
> "Kevin Czinger just handed the entire manufacturing industry the blueprints to the most advanced printed metal chassis ever built. He put them on the internet for free. His argument: the intellectual property isn't the geometry — it's the ability to build it. He's right. I've spent two days inside the dataset. There are three things in there that changed how I'm thinking about the Turbio's subframe."

*Reader action: "wait, what three things?" → forward to 3 people.*

---

### DEAD
> "Our V12 engine is being developed by Italtecnica, a premier Italian engine manufacturer with decades of experience in high-performance automotive engines."

### ALIVE
> "There are eleven men in a building outside Bologna who have spent the last fourteen months making a V12 engine sound like something that doesn't exist yet. Not louder. Not faster. Different. The chief engineer told me he tunes the intake runners the way his grandfather tuned church organs — by listening to the harmonics until the air column sings. I asked him what frequency. He laughed. 'If I could tell you the frequency, a computer could do it.'"

---

### DEAD
> "The hypercar secondary market has seen premiums of 40-70% above list price, indicating strong demand for limited-production vehicles."

### ALIVE
> "Three cars were allocated last month. Before the paint colour was chosen, all three had offers on the secondary market at 60% above list. The buyers weren't speculating. They were paying for certainty. The certainty of being inside the room before the room was announced. That's a different product than a car. And it means the newsletter you're reading right now is part of the product."

---

**The pattern:** Dead content reports facts. Alive content puts you IN the room. You can hear the engine. You can see the old man's hands. You're standing in the workshop.

---

## Every sentence should pass this test:
# "Can I close my eyes and see this?"
## If the answer is no, it's information. If yes, it's a story.

## 5 Raw Edition Openings — Written to Feel, Not Inform

These aren't outlines. These are actual opening paragraphs. This is the temperature the newsletter should run at.

---

### EDITION CONCEPT 1 — "THE MASTERS" PILLAR

**He hasn't taken a vacation in 40 years.**
**Not because he can't. Because his hands forget.**

Giovanni Rosetti finishes a cigarette outside his workshop in Modena every morning at 6:14. He has done this since 1983. The time matters because his hands need forty minutes of warm-up before they can feel the grain in titanium sheet metal at the resolution he requires. If he sleeps late, the first hour of work is unreliable. He knows this because in 1996 he missed a defect in a panel for Pagani that he would have caught on a normal morning. The defect was invisible to inspection. It was not invisible to him. He hasn't slept past 5:30 since.

Giovanni is one of maybe four people alive who can form compound-curved titanium body panels by hand to aerospace tolerance. He doesn't use the word "aerospace." He says the panel is either right or it's wrong, and he can tell which by running his thumb across the surface with his eyes closed.

He is 73. He has no apprentice. He says he tried to teach two young men but they lacked "the patience to be bored for five years before you understand anything." He isn't sure what happens when he stops.

We are building the Turbio's titanium aero components while Giovanni is still willing to answer the phone.

*Emotional register: Quiet Reverence + Beautiful Sadness + Urgency. The reader feels the weight of time. They feel the loss that's coming. They understand why this car matters NOW.*

---

### EDITION CONCEPT 2 — "THE PERSPECTIVE" PILLAR

**The Bugatti Chiron has 1,578 horsepower and it bores me to death.**

I don't say that to be provocative. I say it because the Chiron is the logical conclusion of a philosophy that has dominated hypercar design for 30 years: more power, more speed, more numbers. And it works. The Chiron is extraordinary. It is also, in a way that's hard to articulate, soulless. It is a number that has been turned into a car.

The question we kept asking ourselves when we started the Turbio was not "how fast?" It was: "what do you feel in the first three seconds of sitting in it, before you've touched anything?" Because that feeling — before the engine starts, before anyone tells you the specs — that's the entire relationship. Every car you've ever loved, you loved before you knew what it could do. You loved the way it made you feel about yourself.

We are building a car for people who already have fast cars. Who already have powerful cars. Who have, in fact, everything. We are building for the absence. The thing they didn't know they were missing until they sat in something that was designed from the first sketch to produce a specific emotion at a specific moment.

I can tell you what that emotion is. But I'd rather you felt it.

*Emotional register: Reframe + Righteous Anger (gentle). This challenges the reader's assumptions about what luxury cars are FOR. Contrarian. Opinionated. The kind of thing a billionaire reads and thinks "finally, someone said it."*

---

### EDITION CONCEPT 3 — "THE INTELLIGENCE" PILLAR

**Joby flew a paying passenger last week.**
**Nobody in the hypercar industry noticed.**
**That's the problem.**

The person who buys a Turbio is the same person who currently takes a helicopter to the airport. In 18 months, they won't. They'll take an eVTOL for a third of the cost, with no rotor noise over the neighbours, and they'll wonder why they ever put up with the helicopter. Joby just got FAA Part 135 air carrier certification — the same threshold that took commercial aviation decades to reach. The ticket was $75. The flight was 14 minutes. That isn't a prototype. That's a product.

I'm writing about this in a car newsletter because no one else in this industry is connecting the dots. The hypercar buyer's world is about to change in ways that have nothing to do with cars. Their commute is changing. Their weekend house access is changing. The radius of where they can live relative to where they work is changing. And if you're building a $2.5M object that lives in that person's world, you'd better understand the world that's coming, not the one that exists today.

Here's what else we're watching this week — and why none of it is what you'd expect in a car company's newsletter.

*Emotional register: Holy Shit + Urgency. Reader thinks: "This company sees around corners. They're not just building a car — they understand my entire life." This is the Stratechery model: intelligence that makes you feel smarter for reading it.*

---

### EDITION CONCEPT 4 — "THE BUILD" PILLAR

**Component 247 failed on Thursday.**
**We're glad it did.**

It was a rear suspension mount. Titanium. Topology-optimised. Printed in a single 14-hour build cycle. It looked beautiful. The algorithm had done exactly what we asked — minimised mass while maintaining stiffness targets at every load path. On paper, it was perfect.

Marco put it in the testing rig at 9am. By 9:47, it had developed a micro-fracture along a lattice junction that the simulation hadn't predicted. The failure mode was unusual — not a stress concentration but a thermal gradient from the print process itself. The geometry was right. The physics of how it was *made* introduced a weakness that existed nowhere in the digital model.

This is exactly the kind of failure that matters to us. Not because we enjoy setbacks. Because this is the 3% that Edition 03 talked about — the gap between what the algorithm knows and what the material actually does under load. A company that trusts the simulation ships that part. We don't.

Marco redesigned the lattice transition overnight. Component 247b printed on Friday. Tested Saturday. It held. The difference between 247 and 247b is the difference between a computer-designed car and a Vittori.

*Emotional register: Holy Shit + Reframe. This is radical transparency about failure — which is the most trust-building content possible for both buyers and investors. "They tell us when things break. That means I can trust them when they say things work."*

---

### EDITION CONCEPT 5 — "THE MASTERS" PILLAR (UNCOMFORTABLE)

**The data was collected without his knowledge.**
**He doesn't care. That's the part I can't stop thinking about.**

Somewhere in northern Italy, sensors have been recording a master craftsman's hand movements, tool pressures, and decision patterns for the last three years. The data feeds a machine learning model that can now replicate 97% of his work. He wasn't asked. The instrumentation was installed as "quality monitoring." When someone finally told him, he said: "Good. At least something will remember."

I've been thinking about that sentence for weeks. "At least something will remember." It's resignation and grace simultaneously. He knows his knowledge is being extracted. He knows the extraction will outlive him. And he's made peace with it — not because he trusts the technology, but because the alternative is that everything he learned in 45 years of work disappears entirely.

This is the conversation the manufacturing industry isn't having loudly enough. Not "will AI replace craftsmen" — that question is already answered. The question is: what happens in the gap between the human leaving and the machine being good enough? Who builds the bridge? And what gets lost in translation?

We build the Turbio in that gap. Deliberately.

*Emotional register: Beautiful Sadness + Reframe + Righteous Anger. This is the kind of piece someone reads and sits with. Not share-worthy in a viral way. Forward-worthy in a "you need to read this" way. This is Patek Philippe-level gravity.*

## The Voice: What Does "Carlos" Sound Like in This Newsletter?

The first-person voice in Edition 03 was perfect. It's not a brand talking. It's a specific person — intelligent, obsessive, a little intense. Someone who visits workshops for fun and stays because something the master said haunted them.

**The voice is:**

- **Sharp without being aggressive.** Precision, not force. A chef's knife, not a chainsaw.
- **Comfortable with darkness.** Willing to talk about failure, loss, and endings. Not everything has to be optimistic.
- **Uncomfortably observant.** Notices things other people don't. The specific time Giovanni finishes his cigarette. The sound Marco's test rig makes.
- **Never selling.** The moment you can hear the sales pitch, the voice is dead. The car is mentioned the way a sculptor mentions the marble — it's the context, not the pitch.
- **Knows when to stop.** Doesn't explain the meaning. Trusts the reader to feel it. The best endings leave something unsaid.
- **Wider than cars.** Connects what's happening in a workshop in Modena to what's happening in AI labs in Munich to what's happening in human civilisation. This isn't an automotive newsletter. It's a manufacturing philosophy newsletter disguised as one.

> *"I visited Enzo's workshop in January. I went because of the crankshaft paper, but I stayed because of something he showed me at the end. He picked up a crankshaft that the sensor system had passed as perfectly balanced. He spun it slowly in his hands, the way a chef tests a pan. He put it down and said: 'This one is wrong. Not much. But wrong.' We sent it back for re-measurement. He was right."*
>
> — This is what the voice sounds like. You can feel the room. You can hear the man. The story does the work.

## What to Steal From the Best Writers (Not the Best Brands)

Stop benchmarking against other luxury newsletters. They're all boring. Benchmark against the best writers alive:

| Writer | What to Steal | Application |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| **Michael Lewis** *(The Big Short, Moneyball)* | Takes impossibly complex systems and makes you understand them through ONE person's story. Never starts with the system. Starts with the human. | Never open with the technology. Open with the person using it. The crankshaft isn't the story. Enzo is the story. |
| **Joan Didion** *(Slouching Towards Bethlehem)* | The precise detail that makes everything else feel real. "The jelly setting in the Mojave sun." One specific image that anchors the entire piece. | Giovanni's cigarette at 6:14am. Marco's test rig at 9:47am. The specific detail is what the reader remembers. |
| **Anthony Bourdain** *(Kitchen Confidential)* | Respect for craft without romanticising it. The kitchen is brutal AND beautiful. | **Ben Thompson** *(Stratechery)* | The single analytical framework applied consistently. ## The 10 Rules, Without Corporate Language

1. **Every edition needs ONE image you can't unsee.** Not a photo. A mental image. The old man spinning the crankshaft. The test rig at 9:47. Giovanni's thumb on the titanium. If there's no image, it's a report.

2. **Open with the person, not the topic.** "Enzo Calabrese, 71, Maranello." Not "The future of crankshaft balancing." The reader meets a human, then discovers the world through their eyes.

3. **Let the reader feel smarter than they were 5 minutes ago.** Every edition should give them something they can say at dinner that makes other people lean in. "Did you know there are only 7 people alive who can..."

4. **Include one thing that has nothing to do with cars.** eVTOL. AI ethics. The philosophy of scarcity. The last foundries. The newsletter is about the WORLD, seen from the workshop.

5. **Don't be afraid of loss.** The best luxury stories have grief in them. Patek Philippe's "Generations" campaign is about mortality. The Enzo story is about disappearing knowledge. This emotional register is what separates luxury from premium.

6. **End before you're done.** The last sentence should leave something unsaid. Not a CTA. Not a summary. A door left open. "I don't know how you encode that. I don't think anyone does."

7. **Be wrong sometimes.** Publish a take. Get pushed back. Respond. A newsletter with no controversy is a newsletter with no readers. "The Bugatti Chiron bores me to death" is the kind of line that starts conversations.

8. **Show the failures.** "Component 247 failed on Thursday." This is the most trust-building content possible. Companies that hide failures look like every other company. Companies that show failures and explain what they learned look like the real thing.

9. **Write like you're telling ONE person.** Not an audience. Not subscribers. One person at a late dinner who just asked "so what exactly are you building?" The best newsletters feel like you're overhearing something private.

10. **Never, ever sound like you're asking for anything.** Not attention, not money, not shares. The newsletter exists because you can't stop thinking about these things. The reader is welcome to listen. That's the entire dynamic.

## 5 Formats That Would Hit Different

### The Phone Call
*"I called Enzo last week. He picked up on the seventh ring. Here's what he said."*

Literally reconstruct a conversation. Not Q&A format — narrative. The reader feels like they're eavesdropping on a call between the founder and a 71-year-old master craftsman. Intimate. Raw. You hear the pauses.

Emotion: Quiet Reverence. Format: 800-1,000 words. Reader feels: "I was in the room."

---

### The Obsessive Detail
*"This is a 1,200-word essay about a single bolt."*

Follow one tiny component — a fastener, a surface finish, a weld — and go absurdly deep. The metallurgy. The tolerance. The person who inspects it. By the end, the reader understands why the car costs what it costs, without anyone saying a number.

Emotion: Holy Shit + Reframe. Format: 1,200 words. Reader feels: "I never knew this was so complex."

---

### The Map
*"There are 14 workshops within 50km of Bologna that together represent 80% of the world's hypercar supply chain. Here they are."*

A geographical essay. The Emilia-Romagna ecosystem. Why this specific 50km radius produced Ferrari, Lamborghini, Maserati, Pagani, Ducati — and now Vittori. Part history, part love letter to a place.

Emotion: Holy Shit + Quiet Reverence. Format: 1,000 words. Reader feels: "I need to visit this place."

---

### The Obituary (Written in Advance)
*"One day, the last person who can do this will stop doing it. This is what will be lost."*

A pre-obituary for a craft, not a person. Hand-beaten aluminum bodywork. Manual engine balancing. Bespoke leather tooling. Written with the weight of something already gone — while the person is still alive. Devastating and motivating.

Emotion: Beautiful Sadness + Urgency. Format: 800 words. Reader feels: "Something important is disappearing and I didn't know."

---

### The Provocation
*"In ten years, there will be no new V12 engines. Here's why we're building the last one."*

A bold, contrarian prediction about the industry — and what Vittori is doing about it. Unapologetically opinionated. The kind of piece people argue about. Arguments are engagement. Engagement is reach.

Emotion: Righteous Anger + Urgency. Format: 600-800 words. Reader feels: "I disagree / I agree so hard I need to send this to 5 people."

## The Only Test That Matters

Forget the 5-test framework. Here's the one test:

# Would someone who will **never buy a $2.5M car** still forward this to a friend?

If yes → the newsletter is working. Because those people amplify the brand to the people who WILL buy the car.

The Enzo crankshaft story passes this test. An engineer in Ohio reads it. A designer in Tokyo reads it. A VC in San Francisco reads it. None of them will buy a Turbio. All of them will forward it. And one of their contacts will.

**That's how a newsletter with 5,000 subscribers sells 50 cars.**

## What to Do Now

- **Feed the AI these 5 example openings as reference tone.** Not as templates to copy — as emotional temperature to match. Say: "The newsletter should feel like these. Not necessarily this style, but this level of emotional intensity and specificity."

- **Kill the word "content" from your vocabulary.** You're not making content. You're writing stories that happen to arrive by email. "Content" is what brands make. Stories are what people read.

- **Write the next edition using Edition Concept 1 or 5 as a starting point.** Pick one of the raw openings above and let the AI run with it. See if it can maintain the temperature for 1,000 words.

- **Add the "uncomfortable" rule.** Every edition should have one moment where the reader goes "oh." Something slightly transgressive, honest, or vulnerable. Showing a failure. Admitting doubt. Saying something the industry doesn't say out loud.
